Fair Work Act · The Difference

Two roads to fair work.

Colorado has been debating how to protect workers from being fired unfairly. The union-backed proposal known as Initiative 43 put the issue on the map. Our Fair Work & Mutual Responsibility Act takes the same goal — ending no-reason firing — and builds it to work for workers and employers, so it can actually last.

Note: Initiative 43 was filed in 2025 and later withdrawn; it is not among the measures certified for Colorado's November 2026 ballot. We compare to it here because it best represents the union-backed just-cause approach. Details below reflect Initiative 43 as filed.

The Question
Initiative 43
Union-backed just-cause proposal
Our Fair Work Act
The balanced approach
Who's protected
Only workers at employers with 9 or more staff. Anyone at a smaller business is left out — creating a cliff at 8.
Every worker, at every employer — no size cutoff. A person at a 7-person shop matters just as much.
Grounds to let someone go
A list of roughly seven enumerated causes (performance, neglect, policy violations, insubordination, misconduct, certain convictions, financial circumstances).
Three clear, honest grounds — conduct, capacity, and genuine operational need — simpler to understand and to follow fairly.
The process
Written notice and grounds within 7 days of the firing.
A written reason within 10 business days, plus a simple Fair Dismissal Checklist — warn, give a chance to improve, document — that, if followed, is a complete defense.
If a firing is unfair
A lawsuit in state court — slow and expensive for a worker and a small employer alike.
A fast, low-cost administrative tribunal modeled on Australia's Fair Work Commission, with conciliation first — built so both sides can actually afford justice.
What it can cost
Back pay plus front pay plus fees — with no cap, so the exposure is open-ended and unpredictable.
A capped, tenure-scaled remedy — a clear maximum of 26 weeks' pay. Predictable for employers, fair for workers.
Legal fees
Awarded to a worker who wins (an employer only if they fully prevail).
Discretionary and two-way — never automatic for either side, which removes one-sided lawsuit leverage.
The safety net underneath
Stands alone — a firing rule with nothing to catch the worker afterward.
Paired with CESA: income continuity, healthcare independent of any job, and retraining — so a fair process leads to a soft landing.

It protects everyone

No 8-employee cliff. A worker at a small business is just as exposed to an unfair firing — and just as deserving of protection — as anyone else.

It keeps employers hiring

Capped, predictable liability and a clear compliance path mean employers aren't afraid to hire. A market where people keep getting hired is a more stable one.

It ends in stability, not just a verdict

Because CESA sits underneath it, a fair process leads to income, healthcare, and retraining — a genuinely more stable life, not just a day in court.

Lend Your Voice

Become a citizen advocate.

You don't have to be a paid insider to move legislation. Colorado lets everyday residents register as volunteer lobbyists — unpaid citizens who can stand up for the bills they believe in. Register with the General Assembly, then help us carry the maveriQ Doctrine right into the committee room.

How it works

  1. Fill out the General Assembly's one-page Volunteer Lobbyist Registration Form.
  2. Receive your lobbyist card — it lets you appear and testify before legislative committees.
  3. Speak up for CESA, the Fair Work Act, and the rest of the Doctrine.
On the form, under “Committee or Lobbyist Group,” list us exactly as:
The Q Collective / Doctrine Policy Group

Volunteer lobbyists are unpaid and register with the Colorado General Assembly — not the Secretary of State — with no monthly disclosures. This is general information, not legal advice; the state's page has the official form and current rules.

Ready to stand up?

Register directly with the Colorado General Assembly using the official form, then let us know you're in and we'll help you get up to speed on the bills before you ever testify.
